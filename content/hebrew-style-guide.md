# Hebrew Style Guide — Know Thyself Prompts

This document defines the repeatable process for crafting Hebrew prompts.
It unblocks the 30-question seed bank (issues #21, #25).

---

## Authoring Language

Prompts are **written originally in Hebrew**.

English drafts may be used for brainstorming, but the published prompt is always authored (not translated) in Hebrew. Translation flattens tone; native Hebrew writing preserves the directness and rhythm the product needs.

---

## Tone Calibration

The Hebrew register sits between **spoken educated** (שפה מדוברת משכילה) and **clean written** (שפה כתובה נקייה).

Think: how a thoughtful friend would phrase a question over coffee — not a therapist, not a professor, not a self-help book.

| ✅ Target | ❌ Avoid |
|---|---|
| Direct second-person (אתה/את) | Impersonal forms (האדם, המשתמש) |
| Present tense, active voice | Passive or nominalized constructions |
| Everyday vocabulary | Academic Hebrew (שפה גבוהה) |
| Short, clear sentences | Complex subordinate clauses |

Gender: use masculine singular as the default form (standard for Hebrew UI/product copy). Future versions may offer a gender toggle.

---

## Sentence-Length Rules

- **Target: 4–10 words per prompt** (Hebrew words, not English equivalents).
- Maximum: 15 words. If a prompt exceeds 15, split it or cut.
- One sentence is the ideal. Two sentences only when the second adds necessary framing.
- No colons, semicolons, or em-dashes within prompts. Period or question mark only.

---

## Vocabulary Boundaries

### Use freely
- Concrete nouns and common verbs
- Emotion words from daily speech (כועס, שמח, מפחד, גאה)
- Simple temporal markers (היום, השבוע, לאחרונה)

### Use sparingly
- Abstract nouns (תודעה, משמעות, זהות) — one per prompt maximum
- Loanwords — only when the Hebrew alternative is awkward (e.g., אינטואיציה is fine)

### Avoid entirely
- Therapy/clinical jargon: טראומה, מנגנון הגנה, הדחקה, העברה, טריגר
- Academic tone: באספקלריה של, בהינתן ש, ניתן לומר כי
- Self-help clichés: לצאת מאזור הנוחות, להתחבר לעצמך, לגלות את הפוטנציאל
- English words written in Hebrew letters (unless universally adopted)
- Rhyming or poetic affectation

---

## Structural Patterns

Good prompts follow one of these shapes:

1. **Direct question** — מה ___ ? / מתי ___ ? / למה ___ ?
2. **Complete the thought** — ה___ שאתה ___ הוא ___
3. **Binary choice** — ___ או ___ ? למה?
4. **Observation prompt** — תאר ___ שקרה ___

Avoid:
- Multi-part questions (save those for the 10% deep-dive extension)
- Instructions disguised as questions ("חשוב על רגע ש...")
- Questions that require external research or recall of specific facts

---

## Review Process

Every prompt goes through three stages before entering the seed bank:

### 1. Draft
Write the Hebrew prompt. Read it aloud. If it sounds like a textbook or a therapist, rewrite.

### 2. Gut Check
Answer the prompt yourself in under 2 minutes. If you can't start writing within 10 seconds, the prompt is unclear or too abstract.

### 3. Peer Review
At least one other person (ideally a native Hebrew speaker who is not involved in the project) reads the prompt cold and says back what they think it's asking. If their interpretation diverges from intent, revise.

---

## Quality Checklist (per prompt)

- [ ] Written natively in Hebrew (not translated)
- [ ] 4–15 Hebrew words
- [ ] One idea only
- [ ] No therapy jargon or self-help clichés
- [ ] Answerable in 2–3 minutes
- [ ] Passes the "read aloud" test — sounds human
- [ ] Tagged with 1–2 themes from the approved tag set
- [ ] Classified as Unique or Evergreen
